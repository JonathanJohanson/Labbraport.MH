This report investigates the moment of inertia of rotating bodies using experimental measurements of angular velocity as a function of time. 
Linear regression was used to determine the angular acceleration from the measured data, 
and the results were compared for different experimental configurations. 
The aim was to examine how the distribution of mass affects the moment of inertia and to evaluate how well the experimental results agree with theoretical expectations.
\\\\
The collected data showed good linearity and values not far from those which could be expected.
Slight deviations were however observed and their origin could not be determined to be from either the methods used
or the fact that the system deviated slighly from the theoretical system used for the calculations in for instance shape. 
To determine the origin of the observed deviation, it was concluded that further reasearch would be needed.

Moment of inertia is a physical property of anything which rotates, characterized by the resistance
to changes in angular velocity. Since this affects how much force needs to be applied to accelerate or
decelerate objects, knowing how to calculate and measure it is crucial for many applications. In this study several
means of measuring the moment of inertia practically were explored and compared with theoretical values. The aim was
to determine if this was a valid means of measuring the moment of inertia and how accurate of a method it was. 
A second goal of the experiment was to determine the effect of impulse on a rotating system to show
with a practical example that angular momentum is conserved in systems like this while angular kinetic energy is not.

To calculate with rotational movement and cannot idealize the mass to a particle as often done in translational calculations,
you have to use expressions 
giving a link between the mass of an object and how it is distributed \parencite[339]{up}

$I = moment\: of \:inertia$, different geometric forms will give different calculations 
about its centroid being the center of the distributed mass.\\\\
Examples of equations of moment of inertia
\\\\
Equation for a circular plate
\begin{equation}
I_x=I_y=m\frac{r^2}{2}
\label{Iplate}
\end{equation}
\parencite[342]{up}
\\\\
Equation for a circular ring
\begin{equation}
I_x=I_y=\frac{1}{2}m(r^2_1+r^2_2)
\label{Iring}
\end{equation}
\parencite[342]{up}
\\\\
Using this link Newton's second law $\sum F=ma$ analogously can be applied for rotational movement \parencite[335]{up}. 
\begin{equation}\sum \tau = I\alpha\end{equation}

$\tau$ equals torsion $I$ equals moment of inertia and $\alpha$ the angular acceleration \\\\

To calculate torque ($\tau$) it is the distance to where the applied force perpendicular to the rotational center.  
\begin{equation}\tau=Tr\end{equation}
\parencite[171]{ph}\\\\
T is the applied force in Newton and r is the distance to the point of perpendicular applied force.\\\\ 
Given an expression for $\tau=Tr$ the two formulas can be joined to 
\begin{equation}Tr=I\alpha\end{equation}
In the case that the force is generated with a freely falling weight the following substitutions can be made $\sum F=mg-T=ma$.  
a = the linear acceleration of the falling mass giving the expression. 
\begin{equation}mgr=I\alpha\end{equation}
When the centroid of an object and the axes of rotation do not align The Parallel Axis Theorem (Steiner's theorem) can be used to calculate the moment of inertia.

\begin{equation}I_{total}=I_{cm}+mr^2\:(r=the\,distance\,between\,axis)\end{equation}
\parencite[181]{ph}
\\\\
\begin{comment}
Tourqe are given by T times the distance to the perpendicular line the force are aplied on 
\begin{equation}Tr=\tau\end{equation}
\parencite[171]{ph}
\end{comment}

With the given equations an expression for the experimental moment of inertia can be derived.
\begin{equation}
I=\frac{mgr}{\alpha}-mr^2
\label{finalderivation}
\end{equation}

For calculations of angular momentum $L$ and rotational energy $k_{rot}$  formulas analogous to linear motion are used. 
\begin{equation}
E_{rot}=\frac{1}{2}\omega I
\label{Erot}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
L=I\omega    
\label{L}
\end{equation}    
\parencite[181]{ph}
\\\\
For error estimation the standard deviation can be calculated to give a sense of how close to the mean value the measurements are distributed.
$x_i$ is the experimental value, x the mean value and n the amount of data collections 
\begin{equation} 
S=\sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-x)^2}{n-1}}
\label{deviat}
\end{equation}
\parencite[463]{ph}

Two different experiments were conducted one to experimentally determine the moment of inertia and one to 
see what happens with angular momentum and rotational energy after an impact with an additional body following along with the rotation.  
\\\\
Both experiments had a common setup with disk, ring, and the weight applying the force in the system. 
The weight disk and ring were weighed on a laboratory scale and the measurements on the ring and disk were made with a vernier caliper.
The experiments were conducted using the PASCO Capstone system with the sensor setup shown in figure \ref{fig:labsetup}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{labsetup.png}
    \caption{Graphic showing the sensor setup system.}
    \label{fig:labsetup}
\end{figure}

The data was logged with angular velocity and time on the y and x axis in the Pasco system. 
To have controlled force applied in the system a small weight attached to the disk in the sensor system was used. The weight was released and the PASCO system recorded the event, for the first 
experiment looking at experimental moment of inertia the disk alone and with an additional ring weight added to the system as in figure \ref{fig:labsetup} was measured five times.\\\\
experiment two looking at angular momentum and kinetic energy was conducted in a similar manner but with the ring dropped upon the disk while the weight was still falling. 
The distance from the approximated ring center to the center of the disk was measured with a vernier caliper.

All the data show good linearity, indicating that the use of a linear model was indeed appropriate,
while forces such as air resistance are exponential in nature they were not of a sufficient magnitude
to significantly affect the results. Additionally, none of the data strayed from the theoretical values
by completely unexpected amounts such as an order of magnitude. The observed moments of inertia did 
however also not completely align with the expected values, differing by 1.005 $kgm^{2}\cdot 10^{-4}$ 
which is not insignificant when the observed value is only $7.393 kgm^{2}\cdot 10^{-4}$. It cannot, however,
be ruled out that this difference could stem from the fact that the samples tested were not perfect cylinders,
the system not being completely frictionless, or similar factors. In this case the observed result could be
closer to the actual value than the theoretical value, which assumes perfect conditions. 
\\\\
Whether this deviance is to be considered significant depends on the application. If for instance the aim is
to ensure a motor in a stationary application has sufficient torque, this method is totally adequate as the 
resulting moment of inertia is higher than the theoretical one. Dimensioning with these experimental values
should therefore always ensure some limited level of safety margin, without further testing, this should 
however not be relied upon and instead be used in conjunction with additional safety margins.
\\\\
To find whether the experimentally determined moment of inertia is a better or worse approximation of the actual
value than the theoretical one a more accurate model taking into consideration the exact shape of the disk and
friction would need to be developed to compare against. Alternatively similar experiments could be run with multiple
other shapes to determine the deviation from expected values in those cases, which would give an indication of
how much variance comes from the imperfections in measurements versus how much comes from the variance in shape between
the actual sample and the one used in the theoretical calculations.
\\\\
The results of the experiment involving impulse do show the conservation of angular momentum however gave less conclusive
results. The angular momentum showed a change of 2.985\% after the impact while the kinetic energy showed a change of 2.949\%.
These values are so similar that they can essentially be regarded as the same. Which does not tell anything about the conservation
of angular momentum compared to rotational kinetic energy. It does however show that the change in angular momentum is relatively small
and can likely be attributed to losses caused by the system being imperfect.
\\\\
To better observe this difference and get clearer data that angular momentum is indeed conserved, a more ideal test would need to be conducted
such as using a disk with a higher friction surface to minimize sliding between disc and ring. Or dropping the ring from a jig which holds
is perfectly centered over the disc.
